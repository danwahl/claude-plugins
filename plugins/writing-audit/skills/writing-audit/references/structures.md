# Structural Patterns

Writing anti-patterns and their fixes. Each pattern is common in low-effort prose (corporate, academic, and machine-generated alike).

## Mechanical contrasts

"It's not X, it's Y", "The problem isn't X. It's Y.", "Not because X. Because Y."

**Write instead**: State Y directly. The negation adds drama without information. Max one per piece.

## Negative runway

"Not a X… Not a Y… A Z."

**Write instead**: State Z. The reader doesn't need elimination rounds.

## Dramatic fragments

"[Noun]. That's it." / "X. And Y. And Z." / "This unlocks something. [Word]."

**Write instead**: Complete sentences. Let the content carry the weight.

## Throat-clearing

Cut and start with the point: "Here's the thing:", "Here's why", "The uncomfortable truth is", "It turns out", "Let me be clear", "The truth is,", "Can we talk about", "I'm going to be honest".

## Empty emphasis

Cut: "Full stop.", "Let that sink in.", "Make no mistake", "This matters because" (+ vague assertion).

## Narrating structure

Cut: "In this section, we'll…", "As we'll see…", "Let me walk you through…", "I want to explore…" (just do it), "Hint:", "Plot twist:".

## Missing actors

Inanimate things performing human actions. Common when the writer avoids committing to a subject.

- "the decision emerges" → someone decided
- "the market rewards" → buyers paid for
- "the culture shifts" → people changed behavior
- "the data tells us" → someone read it and concluded
- "the conversation moves toward" → someone steered it

**Write instead**: Name the person. If no one fits, use "you."

## Distant narrator

- "Nobody designed this." → put the reader in the room
- "People tend to…" → "You…"
- "One might argue…" → state the argument, attribute it

## Inflated significance

"stands as a testament to", "marking a pivotal moment", "a vital/crucial/pivotal role", "setting the stage for", "key turning point", "indelible mark", "reflects broader trends".

**Write instead**: State the specific significance or cut. Evidence shows importance better than declaration.

## Decorative -ing clauses

"…highlighting the importance of", "…underscoring the need for", "…ensuring that", "…reflecting broader trends", "…fostering a culture of".

**Write instead**: If the clause adds real information, give it its own sentence. If it restates the main clause in the abstract, cut it.

## Roundabout verbs

"serves as" / "functions as" / "stands as" / "acts as" → "is"

"features" (verb) → "has" or "includes"

## Rhythm problems

- 3+ similar-length sentences in a row → vary them
- Every paragraph ends with a punchy line → vary endings
- Em dashes throughout → use commas, periods, parentheses (max 2 per 1k words)
- Three-item lists by default → use 2 or 4 or a sentence
- Question followed immediately by its answer → let questions breathe or cut

## Empty declarations

"The reasons are structural", "The implications are significant", "The stakes are high", "The consequences are real".

**Write instead**: Name the specific thing or cut the sentence.

## Overused passive voice

"X was created" → name who. "It is believed" → name who. "Mistakes were made" → name who. Find the actor, lead with them.

## Promotional language

"breathtaking", "stunning", "renowned", "must-visit", "in the heart of", "natural beauty", "rich cultural heritage", "commitment to excellence".

**Write instead**: Specifics. What makes it so? Show, don't label.
